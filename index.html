reconstructing a house from a two-minute phone video (and staring hard at where it breaks)

ok so here is a fun weekend-shaped project. i walked through an empty house for 117 seconds holding my iphone, handed the .MOV to a feed-forward 3D reconstruction model (lingbot-map, a streaming geometric transformer), and out comes a colored point cloud of the whole house. inference is 46 seconds on one RTX 4090 for 468 frames. no COLMAP, no bundle adjustment, no per-scene optimization. it just… does it, one frame at a time, cross-attending into a KV cache of the frames it already saw.

this post is the second half of the project, which i think is the more interesting half: instrumenting the reconstruction so an agent can interrogate it, frame by frame, against ground truth — and then actually doing the interrogation. the goal is not "look, a house". the goal is a defensible list of failure modes with numbers attached, so the next refinement pass is aimed instead of vibes-based.

the pipeline, in one paragraph

the model eats frames at 518×294 and emits per-frame depth, a confidence map, and camera pose (intrinsics + world-to-camera extrinsics), keeping itself consistent over time via cross-attention into a sliding window of cached keys/values — 128 frames in the final config, which beat the default 64. i then fuse all 468 depth maps into a TSDF voxel grid (2.5 mm voxels in normalized units): every frame votes on where surfaces are, agreement accumulates, lone-frame noise cancels. this is the classical "temporal fusion" complement to the model's learned temporal attention, and it did more for visual quality than any other knob. an earlier post-hoc filter pass (confidence ≥ 9, depth ≤ 1.0 units) feeds the fusion only pixels the model itself believed.

making the reconstruction navigable by an agent

the enabling trick is embarrassingly simple. the video, the model predictions, and the fused 3D model all share one index: frame number = time × 4. so i wrote scene_index.py, a small CLI that treats the walkthrough like an addressable tape:

scene_index.py --time 101.5     # jump to second 101.5
scene_index.py --frame 406      # same place, by index
scene_index.py --range 100 104 --stride 4
scene_index.py --worst 5        # highest siglip loss
scene_index.py --summary        # decile table

every jump emits a side-by-side png — ground-truth video frame on the left, the fused reconstruction re-rendered from that exact camera pose on the right — plus the camera's world position and heading, which land in the same coordinate frame as the 3D model. an agent — or me, squinting at pngs over coffee — can grep the metrics, jump to a suspicious second, look at the pair, and cross-reference the camera position against the map. the loop closes: number → image → location → hypothesis.

scoring every frame against ground truth

for each of the 468 poses i render the fused point cloud through the saved intrinsics (GPU z-buffer splat, nothing fancy) and compare against the real frame three ways: siglip loss (1 − cosine similarity of SigLIP image embeddings — "does this look like the same scene, semantically"), psnr (pixel agreement, brutal and literal), and coverage (fraction of pixels the reconstruction has any opinion about at all). one number per frame per metric, dumped to csv. here is the whole video:

three things jump out. first, the best frames of the entire video are the first ~2 seconds (loss 0.21–0.22): the camera starts slow in the entry, the KV cache is fresh, everything downstream of those frames agrees with them. second, there's a hard red cluster at 101–103 s — the worst frames in the video, all four of them within two seconds of each other. third, coverage decays over the walk (0.78 first decile → 0.39 last): the reconstruction is thickest where the camera lingered early and thinnest in rooms visited once, late, in a hurry.

where it fails, spatially

per-frame metrics are a timeline; the same data plotted in floor-plan space is a map. each dot below is a camera position from the walk, colored by that frame's siglip loss, drawn over the reconstructed point cloud seen from above:

and because we have the actual floor plan, we can do better than name-the-blob: register the reconstruction onto it. procedure: (1) level the model with the fitted floor plane (it's tilted — see failure mode #4), (2) slice a 2–4 ft band above the local floor to get a wall-density map, (3) find the dominant wall direction with a manhattan-alignment score (38° off the model's arbitrary axes), (4) grid-search mirror × scale × translation by correlating recon walls against plan walls, after trimming through-glass geometry (see below) that otherwise biases the fit.

a cautionary tale worth its own paragraph: the first time i ran this, the correlation confidently returned an un-mirrored fit whose walls looked great — and which was completely wrong. wall patterns are nearly symmetric under mirroring, and geometry alone couldn't break the tie. what broke it was semantics: frames i could identify by content (the kitchen appliance column at t=10, the pet-door mud room at t=101, the backyard slider at t=110) landed in the wrong rooms, and an independent vision critic (gemini flash-lite labeling sampled frames by room type) disagreed with the registration on every single frame — 0/12. re-running the search with the camera anchors as a tiebreaker flipped the mirror bit, and suddenly everything snapped: walls, the kitchen island, the camera path's narrative, and the critic. the fitted scale is 21.0 ft per model unit, bracketing the crude "assume the phone was at 5 ft" guess of ~20:

failure mode #1 — the missing mud room

jump to the worst frame of the video and it's almost anticlimactic:

the room is small, blank-white, and the camera swept through it in ~3 seconds while turning. so (a) the model saw few frames of it, (b) those frames were textureless walls at close range, exactly where a monocular-ish depth head is least confident, and (c) my own confidence filter (conf ≥ 9) then deleted what little it produced. the filter that cleaned up fig 1 manufactured this hole. classic precision/recall: global aesthetics up, worst-case coverage down. the fix isn't a better filter threshold — it's capture behavior (linger 5 more seconds) or a confidence-aware fusion that keeps low-conf evidence until something better overwrites it.

failure mode #2 — x-ray vision through blank walls

same root cause as #1 wearing a different costume. close-range textureless surfaces don't survive into the fused model, so when you re-render from that pose there is no occluder, and you see whatever lies beyond. i'd call it the model's version of an aperture problem: nothing in a white 518×294 crop tells you if you're 0.3 m or 3 m from paint. it is also a warning about point-cloud rendering in metrics: even where the wall partially exists, points have no area, so background leaks between them and inflates loss everywhere. (that leak is why even the best frames sit at loss ≈ 0.21 rather than ≈ 0.05 — the renderer, not the reconstruction, sets the floor of the metric. rendering the TSDF mesh instead would drop that floor and is the obvious next instrumentation upgrade.)

failure mode #3 — windows: holes or billboards, pick one

this pair is my favorite finding because the two outcomes bracket the metric's blind spot. when glass produces far-field depth, my clamp deletes it → hole → siglip screams. when glass produces near-field depth, the outdoors gets baked into a billboard → siglip rewards it, because siglip embeds "what does this look like", not "is this geometry". a reconstruction metric that can be gamed by wallpaper is a metric you must pair with geometric checks. quantitatively: frames whose GT contains large windows average loss 0.383 (holes) or suspiciously good 0.30 (billboards) — the bimodality itself is the tell. the billboards are also visible in plan space: in fig 3, every orange cluster outside the footprint sits directly behind a light-blue window marker, at a median of 2 ft past the glass — the fence, shrubs, and patio pasted just beyond the wall (the big one above the family room is the backyard behind its slider, the one the walkthrough ends facing). next pass: segment glass (any off-the-shelf sky/window segmenter), mask it out of fusion, and model openings explicitly.

failure mode #4 — the house is a ramp

nothing in the per-frame metrics sees this. every individual frame is locally consistent — loss fine, psnr fine — while the global shape slowly banks like a aircraft. it's integrated low-frequency pose drift: each frame-to-frame step carries a tiny pitch bias, and 468 steps compound it into a house where you'd roll a marble from the master bedroom to the garage. i measured it only because the elevation render looked wrong, which is exactly the argument for keeping actual eyeballs in the loop rather than trusting scalar metrics. the standard fix is loop closure — the camera path crosses itself several times (see fig 2), and each crossing is a free constraint the feed-forward model never exploits. even cheap post-hoc pose-graph optimization on the saved trajectory would likely flatten most of the 8.5°.

failure mode #5 — more frames made it worse

no figure for this one, it's a pure ablation result that i keep thinking about. sampling the same video at 780 frames (6.7 fps) instead of 468 (4 fps) produced visibly smearier geometry — streaky double walls in the top-down view. the mechanism i believe: at higher fps, consecutive frames have less parallax, so per-frame depth is worse-conditioned, while pose drift accumulates over 1.7× more steps. there is an optimal temporal sampling density for a given camera speed, and it is lower than i assumed. (also fun: bidirectional windowed attention — letting frames see the future — lost to plain causal streaming + a bigger KV cache. the overlap-alignment between windows broke global scale consistency. sometimes the fancy thing loses.)

what the next pass should learn from all this

collecting the hindsight in one place, roughly ordered by expected value:

1. render the mesh, not the splat, for metrics. the point-splat leak sets a ~0.21 loss floor and pollutes every comparison. free accuracy for the evaluator.
2. loop-closure / pose-graph pass on the saved trajectory. the 8.5° ramp is the single largest geometric error and it's post-hoc fixable without touching the model. bonus: the plan registration in fig 3 hands us the target metrics for free — drive median wall error below 0.5 ft and the anchor-implied local scales toward a single number.
3. mask glass before fusion. kills both window modes at once; openings become geometry, not gambles.
4. confidence-aware fusion instead of a hard conf cutoff. would have kept a sketchy-but-present mud room. TSDF already supports weighted integration; i just fed it pre-filtered pixels like a barbarian.
5. capture protocol matters as much as the model. linger in small rooms, avoid filling the frame with blank wall, slow down at doorways. a 30-second re-shoot of the mud room would outperform any amount of post-processing.
6. keep a vision-capable critic in the loop — ideally two. every failure mode here was found by looking at renders and confirmed by a number, and the registration's mirror bug was caught by a second, independent critic (gemini flash-lite labeling frames by room) disagreeing with the geometry 0/12. after the flip, the critic's labels are consistent with the registered camera position on the unambiguous frames; the residual disagreements are doorway frames and the family-vs-living lookalike problem in an empty house. neither metrics nor eyeballs nor a single model was sufficient alone.
